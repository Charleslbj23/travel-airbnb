Conclusions & Findings

So to wrap everything up, what did we actually find? Airbnb in Los Angeles is not random, and it is not evenly shared. Listings pile up in tourist neighborhoods like Hollywood, Venice, and Long Beach. The vast majority of those listings are entire homes, meaning full residential units taken off the market for visitors. And a small group of commercial operators controls most of the supply.

Our data has limits. We cannot see confirmed bookings, actual revenue, or the lived experience of someone who got priced out of their neighborhood. Those silences in the data are real, and they matter. But what we can see still tells a pretty clear story. Airbnb in LA looks a lot less like neighbors sharing their homes, and a lot more like a layer of the housing market that benefits commercial operators and the platform, while the costs get distributed across the whole city.

So, for whom?

Our central research question asks: How does Airbnb spatially shape and commodify communities in Los Angeles — and for whom? The evidence across our three arguments consistently points toward an answer that is less about "home sharing" and more about a stratified accommodation market that benefits certain groups disproportionately.

The platform's benefits appear to flow primarily to hosts running multiple listings — operators who treat Airbnb as a business rather than an occasional supplement to their income. These commercial operators, who control nearly two-thirds of all listings, are concentrated in the city's wealthiest and most tourist-accessible neighborhoods. By contrast, residents in lower-income communities who do appear on the platform tend to rely on private-room sharing, a fundamentally different and less profitable relationship with the platform.

The combination of spatial concentration in tourist neighborhoods, the dominance of entire-home listings, and the professionalization of hosting all suggest that the primary beneficiaries of Airbnb's presence in Los Angeles are not ordinary residents sharing spare space, but a smaller group of operators managing properties as assets — alongside the platform itself, which captures a percentage of every transaction.

Limitations & Future Work

This analysis is constrained by what the data can and cannot show. InsideAirbnb captures platform-visible listings and review activity but does not include confirmed booking counts, actual host revenue, guest demographics, or enforcement records. The ACS describes long-term residential populations at the census-tract level but cannot capture tourists, short-term residents, or the lived experience of displacement.

Methodologically, we prevent ecological fallacy by not attributing tract-level demographics to individual hosts or guests. We treat data gaps — like missing booking records and displacement data — as meaningful limits that shape interpretation rather than problems to be solved.

Future work could incorporate enforcement data from the City of LA's Home-Sharing Ordinance compliance records, longitudinal ACS estimates to track demographic change over time, and qualitative interviews with residents in high-density Airbnb neighborhoods to capture the lived experience that quantitative data cannot represent.
