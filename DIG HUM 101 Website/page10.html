Conclusions & Findings

The analysis of Airbnb listings in Los Angeles reveals how digital platforms reshape urban housing markets through listing concentration, housing commodification, and commercial hosting.

First, Airbnb listings are not evenly distributed across the city, they are clustered in tourism-oriented neighborhoods such as Long Beach, Hollywood, Venice, and Santa Monica. This pattern shows that short-term rentals tend to concentrate in existing tourist areas rather than being evenly spread across neighborhoods. Second, the dominance of entire-home listings demonstrates how residential housing units are increasingly converted into short-term accommodations for visitors, transforming homes into revenue-generating assets. Finally, the distribution of listings across hosts reveals that a relatively small group of multi-listing operators controls a large share of available properties, suggesting that Airbnb increasingly operates through professionalized hosting rather than casual home sharing.

Taken together, these patterns illustrate how platform markets can reshape the meaning of housing in cities, blurring the boundaries between residential space, tourism infrastructure, and investment opportunity.

Limitations & Future Work

This analysis is constrained by what the data can and cannot show. InsideAirbnb captures platform-visible listings and review activity but does not include confirmed booking counts, actual host revenue, guest demographics, or enforcement records. The ACS describes long-term residential populations at the census-tract level but cannot capture tourists, short-term residents, or the lived experience of displacement.

Future work could incorporate enforcement data from the City of LA's Home-Sharing Ordinance compliance records, longitudinal ACS estimates to track demographic change over time, and qualitative interviews with residents in high-density Airbnb neighborhoods to capture the lived experience that quantitative data cannot represent.
