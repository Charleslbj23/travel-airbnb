A History of Short-Term Rentals in Los Angeles

Los Angeles has one of the most complex and contested short-term rental histories of any American city. The tensions that Airbnb surfaced — between tourism and tenancy, between property rights and community stability — were not created by the platform. They were inherited from decades of prior policy decisions about who gets to live in Los Angeles and under what conditions.

The foundation of those tensions is the city’s housing stock itself. When the Rent Stabilization Ordinance (RSO) passed in 1978, it capped rent increases on roughly 624,000 units built on or before October 1, 1978 — a legal protection that remains in effect today and that would later become the central mechanism for restricting Airbnb use. The RSO was designed to protect long-term renters from displacement during periods of rapid rent inflation. It also, inadvertently, drew a legal boundary that would later determine which homes could be listed on short-term rental platforms and which could not.

The next major disruption came not from technology but from the state. California’s Ellis Act of 1985 gave landlords the right to remove properties from the rental market entirely, enabling mass evictions in rent-stabilized buildings. Ellis Act evictions surged in later decades as property values rose, accelerating displacement especially in coastal neighborhoods that would later become Airbnb’s most concentrated markets. By the time Airbnb launched in 2008 and arrived meaningfully in Los Angeles around 2012–2014, it entered a housing market already under severe pressure from gentrification, speculation, and a decades-long shortage of affordable units.

Airbnb’s growth in Los Angeles was rapid. By 2015, the platform had enough listings to attract serious policy attention, and city council members began introducing proposed regulations. But Los Angeles did not pass enforceable rules for three years. The Home-Sharing Ordinance, adopted on December 11, 2018, and effective July 1, 2019, ultimately required hosts to register with the city, restricted short-term rentals to primary residences only, capped rentals at 120 days per year (or year-round with extended home-sharing approval), and banned all RSO-covered units from the platform entirely. It was one of the most restrictive short-term rental ordinances in the country.

Enforcement, however, proved uneven. The license field in InsideAirbnb data — which should reflect compliance with the Home-Sharing Ordinance — is frequently blank or populated with placeholder values, suggesting that a significant fraction of listings were operating without valid registration even after the ordinance took effect. The COVID-19 pandemic then upended the market entirely: listings dropped sharply in 2020, a city-wide rent freeze halted evictions, and the tourism economy that sustained coastal Airbnb markets nearly collapsed. Recovery was rapid. By 2022–2023, listing counts had rebounded, and the platform’s geographic footprint had expanded.

This history matters for interpreting our data. The patterns we observe — clustering in coastal and tourist-adjacent neighborhoods, dominance of entire-home listings, high rates of multi-listing commercial operators — are not random. They reflect accumulation: of capital, of policy gaps, of displacement pressure that preceded the platform by decades.

Key Policy Moments

  • 1978 — Rent Stabilization Ordinance (RSO): Los Angeles enacts the RSO, capping rent increases on approximately 624,000 units built on or before October 1, 1978. RSO protections will later prohibit those units from short-term rental use entirely.
  • 1985 — California Ellis Act: State law allows landlords to permanently remove properties from the rental market, enabling mass evictions of rent-stabilized tenants. Ellis Act evictions accelerate displacement in neighborhoods that later become Airbnb hotspots.
  • 2008 — Airbnb Founded: Airbnb launches in San Francisco as an air mattress rental service. The platform expands nationally and arrives meaningfully in Los Angeles around 2012–2014.
  • 2015–2018 — Regulatory Debate: Multiple proposed regulations circulate through the LA City Council without passage. Housing advocates and industry lobbyists contest the scope and enforcement mechanisms of any future ordinance.
  • December 11, 2018 — Home-Sharing Ordinance Adopted: Los Angeles Municipal Code § 12.22 A.32 is passed, requiring host registration, limiting rentals to primary residences, capping stays at 120 days per year, and banning all RSO units from the platform.
  • July 1, 2019 — Home-Sharing Ordinance Takes Effect: The ordinance becomes enforceable. Compliance is immediately uneven: a substantial share of active listings show no valid registration number in platform data.
  • March 2020 — COVID-19 Pandemic: Los Angeles enters lockdown. Airbnb listings drop sharply. The city enacts a rent freeze preventing evictions, temporarily stabilizing housing costs in RSO-covered units.
  • 2021–2022 — Market Recovery: Tourism rebounds and Airbnb listing counts recover, with the platform’s geographic footprint expanding into previously underrepresented neighborhoods.
  • 2023–2025 — Continued Enforcement Gaps: Despite the Home-Sharing Ordinance, enforcement remains a challenge. InsideAirbnb data shows continued high rates of missing or invalid license numbers, particularly in high-density listing neighborhoods.
