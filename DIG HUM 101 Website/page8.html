Housing Commodification

Airbnb and the commodification of housing

One of the most significant patterns in Airbnb listings across Los Angeles is the dominance of entire-home rentals, which make up the largest share of listings on the platform. Approximately 33,580 listings are categorized as entire homes, far exceeding private-room or shared-room listings. This is important because an entire-home listing represents a complete housing unit that could otherwise be occupied by a long-term resident. Unlike room rentals, where hosts may rent out an extra bedroom while continuing to live in the property, entire-home listings convert full residential properties into short-term accommodation for visitors. Lee (2016) argues that when housing units are converted into Airbnb listings, they are effectively removed from the long-term rental market, leaving fewer homes available for residents and increasing competition for housing.[5]

As shown in Figure 2, entire-home rentals account for the largest share of Airbnb listings across different parts of Los Angeles. In the City of Los Angeles, approximately 74.8% of listings are entire homes, compared to 72.7% in other cities and 71.9% in unincorporated areas. This pattern suggests that Airbnb is used primarily for renting complete housing units rather than spare rooms within occupied homes.[3]

Socioeconomic context from Census data helps illustrate where this process is most visible. Areas with higher household incomes and educational attainment often coincide with places where Airbnb activity is particularly prominent.

In these contexts, housing can easily function as an investment asset capable of generating short-term rental revenue. Empirical research on the LA housing market supports this pattern. Koster, van Ommeren, and Volkhausen (2021) find that Airbnb activity has significantly increased property values in popular areas. In areas within 2.5 km of Hollywood’s Walk of Fame, property values have increased by nearly 15% due to Airbnb activity, while housing near beaches has experienced price increases of about 5.8%.[8] These findings suggest that the economic incentives created by short-term rental platforms encourage property owners to treat housing as a revenue-generating asset. Together, these patterns suggest that Airbnb contributes to the commodification of housing by turning residential properties into short-term rental assets.
