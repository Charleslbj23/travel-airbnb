Commercial Hosting

Who controls Airbnb in Los Angeles?

While the dominance of entire-home listings reveals how housing units are converted into short-term rentals, an equally important question follows: who controls these properties? Examining the distribution of listings across hosts reveals that Airbnb activity in Los Angeles is highly concentrated among a relatively small group of commercial operators. There are approximately 23,005 unique hosts in the dataset, yet only 5,855 hosts (25.5%) manage more than one listing. Despite representing a minority of hosts, these multi-listing operators control 28,435 listings, accounting for roughly 62.4% of all platform supply. The remaining 17,150 hosts (74.5%) each manage a single listing and together account for just 37.6% of total listings.

This imbalance is significant. While Airbnb markets itself as a platform for neighbors sharing their homes, the data tells a different story. A small group of operators controls the majority of supply, suggesting that the platform increasingly functions as a vehicle for professionalized property management rather than casual home sharing (Belasco, 2021). In some cases, individual hosts manage dozens of listings simultaneously, indicating a form of small-scale real estate entrepreneurship operating through the platform rather than genuine peer-to-peer exchange.

Neighborhood-level context from the American Community Survey helps explain where and why these patterns emerge. In many Los Angeles communities, a large share of housing units are renter-occupied, which makes properties more fluid within local housing markets and easier to convert into short-term rental inventory (Cocola-Gant and Guterres, 2021). Research on LA specifically has shown that multi-listing hosts tend to concentrate in neighborhoods where the financial return on short-term rentals far exceeds what long-term tenants would pay, a dynamic that Wachsmuth and Weisler (2018) describe as the platform-driven “rent gap.” In this context, Airbnb does not simply reflect existing housing conditions, it actively reshapes them by making commercial hosting financially attractive in neighborhoods that were previously residential.

Taken together, the concentration of listings among a small group of multi-listing operators suggests that Airbnb in Los Angeles increasingly resembles a commercialized accommodation market. The platform’s visible supply is controlled not primarily by residents sharing spare space, but by operators managing housing as a business, raising important questions about who benefits from the platform economy, and at whose expense.
