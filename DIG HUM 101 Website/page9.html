Argument 3: Commercial Hosting

A relatively small group of multi-listing operators controls the majority of Airbnb listings in Los Angeles — suggesting the platform increasingly operates as a commercialized rental market, not a casual home-sharing service.

Who controls Airbnb in Los Angeles?

While the dominance of entire-home listings shows how housing units are converted into short-term rentals, an equally important question follows: who controls these properties? Examining the distribution of listings across hosts reveals that Airbnb activity in Los Angeles is highly concentrated among a relatively small group of commercial operators. There are approximately 23,005 unique hosts in the dataset, yet only 5,855 hosts (25.5%) manage more than one listing. Despite representing a minority of hosts, these multi-listing operators control 28,435 listings, accounting for roughly 62.4% of all platform supply. The remaining 17,150 hosts (74.5%) each manage a single listing and together account for just 37.6% of total listings.

While Airbnb markets itself as a platform for neighbors sharing their homes, the data tells a different story. A small group of operators controls the majority of supply, suggesting that the platform increasingly functions as a vehicle for professionalized property management rather than casual home sharing.[1] In some cases, individual hosts manage dozens of listings simultaneously, indicating a form of small-scale real estate entrepreneurship operating through the platform rather than genuine peer-to-peer exchange.

Commercial operators: a structural market feature

Commercial operators managing 6 or more listings account for 31–35% of all listings across every neighborhood group. In the City of LA alone, 8,047 listings (35.2%) belong to hosts with 6 or more properties. Roughly one in three Airbnb units in LA operates as part of an informal hospitality business. This is not a fringe phenomenon — it is a structural feature of the market.

Housing context: renter tenure and the rent gap

Neighborhood-level context from the American Community Survey helps explain where and why these patterns emerge. In many Los Angeles communities, a large share of housing units are renter-occupied, which makes properties more fluid within local housing markets and easier to convert into short-term rental inventory.[2] Research on LA specifically has shown that multi-listing hosts tend to concentrate in neighborhoods where the financial return on short-term rentals far exceeds what long-term tenants would pay — a dynamic that Wachsmuth and Weisler[3] describe as the platform-driven “rent gap.” In this context, Airbnb does not simply reflect existing housing conditions; it actively reshapes them by making commercial hosting financially attractive in neighborhoods that were previously residential.

What this means for "for whom?"

Taken together, the concentration of listings among a small group of multi-listing operators suggests that Airbnb in Los Angeles increasingly resembles a commercialized accommodation market. The platform's visible supply is controlled not primarily by residents sharing spare space, but by operators managing housing as a business — raising important questions about who benefits from the platform economy, and at whose expense.

Because guest demographic data is not available in the InsideAirbnb dataset, we use host types as a proxy for understanding who benefits from the platform. The data suggests that the benefits of Airbnb in Los Angeles flow disproportionately to multi-listing operators — hosts who treat the platform not as a way to share their own home, but as a business model. This reframes the "for whom?" question: rather than asking who stays in these listings, we can ask who profits from them — and the answer increasingly points to a small class of professional operators rather than local residents supplementing their income.
