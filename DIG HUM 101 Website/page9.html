Commercial Hosting

Who controls Airbnb in Los Angeles?

While the dominance of entire-home listings reveals how housing units are converted into short-term rentals, an important question follows: who controls these properties? Examining the distribution of listings across hosts reveals that Airbnb activity in Los Angeles is highly concentrated among a relatively small group of commercial operators. There are approximately 23,005 unique hosts in the dataset, yet only 5,855 hosts (25.5%) manage more than one listing. Despite representing a minority of hosts, these multi-listing operators control 28,435 listings, accounting for roughly 62.4% of all platform supply. The remaining 17,150 hosts (74.5%) each manage a single listing and together account for just 37.6% of total listings.

Airbnb markets itself as a platform for neighbors sharing their homes, however, the data suggests a different pattern. A relatively small group of operators controls the majority of listings, indicating that the platform increasingly functions as a system of professionalized property management and not casual home sharing (Belasco 2021).[9] In some cases, individual hosts manage dozens of listings simultaneously, indicating a form of small-scale real estate entrepreneurship rather than genuine peer-to-peer home sharing.

Neighborhood-level data from the American Community Survey helps explain where these patterns appear. In many Los Angeles neighborhoods, a large share of housing units are occupied by renters which can make properties easier to shift between long-term housing and short-term rentals (Cocola-Gant and Guterres 2021).[10] Research on LA also shows that multi-listing hosts tend to concentrate in areas where short-term rentals generate much higher income than traditional long-term rents, a dynamic that Wachsmuth and Weisler (2018) describe as the platform-driven “rent gap.”[1] In this context, Airbnb does not just reflect existing housing conditions; it can also reshape them by making commercial short-term rentals financially attractive in neighborhoods that were previously residential.

Overall, the concentration of listings among a small group of multi-listing operators suggests that Airbnb in LA increasingly functions as a commercial rental market. Instead of residents occasionally sharing extra space, many listings are managed by operators treating housing as a business. This shift raises important questions about who benefits from the platform economy and who bears its costs.
