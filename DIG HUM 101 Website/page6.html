About Our Project

This project was produced as part of DH 101: Introduction to Digital Humanities at UCLA. Our central research question asks: How does Airbnb spatially shape and commodify communities in Los Angeles — and for whom?

To answer this question, we combined two primary datasets: listing-level data from InsideAirbnb, an activist-oriented platform that scrapes publicly visible Airbnb listings, and tract-level demographic data from the U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey (ACS). By spatially joining Airbnb listings to census tracts, we were able to analyze where short-term rental activity concentrates in Los Angeles and how those patterns intersect with neighborhood income, housing tenure, and racial composition.

Our analysis develops three interconnected arguments. First, Airbnb listings are not evenly spread across the city — they cluster heavily in tourism-oriented neighborhoods like Long Beach, Hollywood, Venice, and Santa Monica. Second, the overwhelming dominance of entire-home listings (over 74% of all listings in the City of LA) suggests that short-term rental activity functions primarily as a mechanism for converting residential housing into tourist accommodation. Third, a small group of multi-listing commercial operators controls the majority of platform supply, raising serious questions about who actually benefits from the platform economy.

This project was built using a combination of Python for data processing and analysis, React and JavaScript for interactive data visualizations, and HTML/CSS for the website. The site was designed in Figma, developed in VS Code, and deployed on Vercel.

What went into our project?

Selecting Our Sources

InsideAirbnb provides a high-resolution view of platform-generated housing supply, while the ACS offers a structural view of neighborhood demographics and socioeconomic conditions.

By spatially joining listings (latitude/longitude) to census tracts, we do not claim that area demographics describe individual hosts or guests. Instead, we analyze co-location patterns:

  • Where do entire-home listings cluster relative to income levels?
  • Where do multi-listing hosts appear in relation to rent-burdened areas?
  • Which demographic contexts overlap with high Airbnb density?

If we relied only on InsideAirbnb, Los Angeles would act as a marketplace of listings. If we relied only on the ACS, Los Angeles would act as static demographic zones. By combining them, we analyze how a digital platform economy overlaps and interacts with long-term community structures, helping answer our central research question: How does Airbnb spatially shape and commodify communities in Los Angeles — and for whom?

Processing Our Data

We cleaned and structured the InsideAirbnb listings, standardized price variables, and identified multi-listing hosts. Using geographic coordinates, we corresponded listings to census tracts and joined ACS demographic data. This spatial join allows us to calculate listings per capita, examine entire-home concentration, identify professional host clustering, and compare Airbnb density to neighborhood-level income and housing conditions.

We also observed review timestamps and daily calendar data to compare visible patterns before and after 2020. While these methods do not signify confirmed bookings, they provide a suggestion of platform-visible change over time.

Methodologically, we prevent ecological misconceptions by not attaching tract-level demographics to individual hosts or guests. Instead, we analyze physical co-location patterns. We also treat data gaps—like missing booking records and displacement data—as significant limits that form interpretation.

Presenting Our Narrative

We designed our site in Figma first so we could lock in the layout, spacing, and visual style before writing any code. Then we built the actual website in JavaScript, using VS Code as our IDE to develop and organize the project efficiently.

To publish the site, we deployed it on Vercel, which made hosting straightforward and let us push updates quickly without manually managing servers or file uploads. This workflow—Figma for planning, JavaScript for implementation, VS Code for development, and Vercel for deployment—helped us stay consistent between the design and final product while keeping iteration fast as we refined the site.
