Background

Timeline

This timeline traces the arc of Airbnb's presence in Los Angeles — from the platform's founding in 2008 through nearly two decades of rapid growth, regulatory response, pandemic disruption, and ongoing enforcement challenges. Each event is drawn from our primary datasets (InsideAirbnb and the U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey), published scholarship from our annotated bibliography, and public regulatory records. The timeline contextualizes the spatial and economic patterns we analyze elsewhere on this site by showing when and why they emerged.

Platform & Market Regulation & Policy Crisis & Disruption Data & Research
2008
Platform & Market
August 2008

Airbnb Founded in San Francisco

Brian Chesky, Joe Gebbia, and Nathan Blecharczyk launch "AirBed & Breakfast" from their San Francisco apartment, offering air mattresses to conference attendees. The platform would go on to reshape short-term rental markets in cities worldwide, including Los Angeles.

2009
Data & Research
May 2009

Earliest LA Reviews in Our Dataset

The earliest guest review in the InsideAirbnb Los Angeles dataset appears, marking the platform's initial small-scale foothold in the LA rental market — years before its explosive growth. [12]

2012–2014
Platform & Market
2012–2014

Rapid Growth in LA Listings

Airbnb expands aggressively into Los Angeles. Coastal and tourist-adjacent neighborhoods — Venice, Santa Monica, Hollywood, and Downtown — emerge as early listing hotspots. By 2014, LA is one of Airbnb's largest U.S. markets. Review activity in our dataset accelerates sharply during this period.

2015
Data & Research
2015

InsideAirbnb Launches

Murray Cox, an Australian-American community artist and data activist, creates InsideAirbnb.com to provide publicly accessible data on Airbnb's impact on residential housing. The project begins scraping and publishing listing-level data for cities worldwide, including Los Angeles — creating the primary dataset our project relies on. [1]

2016
Regulation & Policy
July 18, 2016

LA and Airbnb Agree on Tax Collection

The City of Los Angeles and Airbnb announce a deal allowing the platform to collect and remit the city's Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT) from hosts — the city's first formal regulatory acknowledgment of Airbnb's scale in the LA market. [2]

Regulation & Policy
December 7, 2016

Housing Committee Tightens Draft Ordinance

LA's Housing Committee removes a proposed 15-day allowance for non-primary-residence vacation rentals and clarifies the prohibition on rent-controlled (RSO) units being used for short-term rentals. Regulatory momentum toward a comprehensive ordinance builds. [2]

2016–2018
Platform & Market
2016–2018

Listings Nearly Double in LA

The number of short-term Airbnb listings in Los Angeles nearly doubles, driven by rising tourist demand and host profitability. Community pushback intensifies as residents and housing advocates link platform growth to rising rents, reduced housing availability, and neighborhood disruption. [3] [4] [5]

2018
Regulation & Policy
December 11, 2018

LA City Council Passes the Home-Sharing Ordinance

The LA City Council unanimously approves the Home-Sharing Ordinance (LAMC § 12.22 A.32). Hosts may only rent their primary residence, must register with the city and pay a fee, and are capped at 120 days per calendar year. Rent-stabilized units are banned entirely from short-term rental use. [6]

2019
Regulation & Policy
July 1, 2019

Home-Sharing Ordinance Takes Effect

The Home-Sharing Ordinance takes effect and the city's registration portal opens. Hosts must obtain a permit and display their registration number on all listings. Active enforcement does not begin immediately — the city allows a transition period.

Regulation & Policy
November 1, 2019

Enforcement Begins

The City of Los Angeles begins actively enforcing the Home-Sharing Ordinance. Non-registered listings face removal from platforms. One analysis estimates a 78% decrease in short-term rental listings between 2019 and 2022. [8] [9]

Data & Research
July–December 2019

InsideAirbnb Captures the Pre-COVID Baseline

InsideAirbnb scrapes LA listing data in mid- and late 2019, capturing approximately 35,000+ active listings — a critical baseline of the market before both enforcement and the COVID-19 pandemic reshape the STR landscape. [12] [10]

2020
Crisis & Disruption
March 2020

COVID-19 Lockdowns Devastate LA Tourism

California's stay-at-home orders effectively shut down Los Angeles's tourism economy. Airbnb bookings collapse across the city. In our InsideAirbnb review data, review volume drops sharply — one of the most visible traces of the pandemic's impact on short-term rental activity in our dataset.

Crisis & Disruption
Mid-2020

Guest Priorities Shift: Safety Over Community

Airbnb introduces enhanced cleaning protocols and flexible cancellation policies. Research shows guest priorities shift toward physical distancing, entire-unit bookings, self-check-in, and location privacy. Spatial attributes that were once secondary become commodified as premium safety features. [11]

Platform & Market
December 2020

Airbnb IPO

Airbnb goes public on NASDAQ at a valuation of approximately $47 billion — despite the ongoing pandemic. The IPO signals investor confidence in long-term demand for short-term rentals and further entrenches the platform's structural role in urban housing markets.

2021–2022
Platform & Market
2021–2022

Post-COVID Recovery Reshapes the Market

Review activity in our InsideAirbnb data rebounds as travel resumes, but the post-pandemic market looks fundamentally different from the pre-2019 peak. The combined effects of the Home-Sharing Ordinance, enforcement, and shifting host economics mean some neighborhoods recover faster than others.

Crisis & Disruption
November 2021–October 2022

The Enforcement Gap

City data reveals a stark enforcement gap: more than 2,000 non-compliant short-term rentals were advertised each month, yet only 27 fines were issued across the entire year. Had the city fined each non-compliant host just once in July 2022, it would have generated nearly $3.5 million in revenue. [7]

2023
Data & Research
2023

Housing Justice Data Lab Founded

Murray Cox establishes the nonprofit Housing Justice Data Lab (HJDL) to continue maintaining InsideAirbnb, ensuring ongoing data collection and public access for researchers, journalists, and community advocates. [1]

2025
Data & Research
2025

Our Dataset's Full Coverage Window

Our InsideAirbnb review data extends through December 2025, giving the project a 16-year window of observable activity (May 2009–December 2025). Combined with ACS 5-Year Estimates at the census-tract level (DP05, S0101, S1901, S1501, S2503), this allows us to trace platform growth, regulatory disruption, COVID impacts, and post-pandemic recovery — layered against the demographic structure of LA's communities. [12] [13]
