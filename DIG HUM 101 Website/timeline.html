Background

Timeline

This timeline traces key events that shaped the data we analyze in this project. Each entry either describes something directly observable in our InsideAirbnb and American Community Survey datasets, or documents a policy, crisis, or market shift whose effects are visible in the spatial and temporal patterns our data records.

Platform & Market Regulation & Policy Crisis & Disruption Data & Research
1978–79
Regulation & Policy 1978–1979

LA Enacts the Rent Stabilization Ordinance

Los Angeles caps rent increases for roughly 624,000 units built on or before October 1978. The RSO matters for our project because the Home-Sharing Ordinance later bans every RSO unit from short-term rental use entirely. When we look at our InsideAirbnb listings data, that ban helps explain why listing density clusters in some census tracts and stays low in others. [1]

1985
Regulation & Policy 1985

California Passes the Ellis Act

The Ellis Act gives landlords the right to evict tenants and pull properties off the rental market. Since 2001, over 27,000 rent-controlled units in LA have disappeared through Ellis evictions, and a portion of those ended up as Airbnb rentals. For our project, this connects the housing loss we track in ACS data (S2503 housing cost burden, DP05 demographic shifts) to the short-term rental supply that shows up in InsideAirbnb. [2] [3]

2009
Data & Research May 2009

Earliest LA Review in Our Dataset

The first guest review in our InsideAirbnb Los Angeles reviews.csv shows up this month. LA's short-term rental market is basically nonexistent at this point, but this single timestamp is what anchors the start of our 16-year analysis window. [4]

2012–14
Platform & Market 2012–2014

Airbnb Explodes Across Los Angeles

Review activity in our InsideAirbnb data picks up fast as Airbnb pushes into Los Angeles. Venice, Santa Monica, Hollywood, and Downtown all emerge as early listing hotspots, and these are the same neighborhoods that show up as high-density clusters in our spatial analysis later on. By 2014, LA is already one of Airbnb's biggest U.S. markets. [5] [6]

2015
Data & Research 2015

InsideAirbnb Launches

Murray Cox, a community artist and data activist, launches InsideAirbnb.com to make Airbnb's impact on residential housing publicly visible. The site starts scraping and publishing listing-level data for cities around the world, Los Angeles included. This becomes the primary dataset our project uses for listing location, room type, host behavior, pricing, and review activity. [7]

2016–18
Platform & Market 2016–2018

Listings Nearly Double

Short-term Airbnb listings in LA nearly double during this stretch. Our data shows the highest wave of new host registrations (visible through host_since dates in listings.csv) alongside accelerating review volume. Researchers identify this as the peak of unregulated Airbnb expansion in the city, and housing advocates start drawing direct lines between platform growth, rising rents, and shrinking housing availability. Our ACS income (S1901) and housing cost burden (S2503) tables let us track those dynamics at the tract level. [8] [9] [10]

2018
Regulation & Policy December 11, 2018

Home-Sharing Ordinance Passed

The LA City Council unanimously passes the Home-Sharing Ordinance (LAMC § 12.22 A.32). Under the new rules, hosts can only rent their primary residence, everyone has to register and pay a fee, rentals are capped at 120 days per year, and RSO units are banned from short-term use entirely. The license field in our InsideAirbnb listings data, which is often blank or missing, ends up reflecting just how uneven compliance really is. [11]

2019
Regulation & Policy July 1, 2019

Ordinance Takes Effect

The Home-Sharing Ordinance officially goes live. The city opens its registration portal and requires hosts to display their permit number on every listing. In our InsideAirbnb data, the license field starts filling in for some listings around this time, but a large number stay blank. We map that gap to see where compliance is actually happening and where it isn't.

Regulation & Policy November 1, 2019

Active Enforcement Begins

Non-registered listings face removal from platforms. One analysis estimates a 78% decrease in listings between 2019 and 2022. In our data, this regulatory shock is visible as a drop in active listing counts and a shift in the geographic distribution of remaining listings. [12] [13]

Data & Research July–December 2019

The Pre-Disruption Baseline

InsideAirbnb scrapes LA listing data in mid- and late 2019, capturing around 35,000+ active listings. This turns out to be the critical pre-disruption baseline for our analysis, since it's the last clean look at the market before enforcement and the pandemic both hit. Review activity in our dataset peaks right around here. [4] [14]

2020
Crisis & Disruption March 2020

COVID-19 Shuts Down LA Tourism

California's stay-at-home orders shut down LA's tourism economy almost overnight. Monthly review counts in our InsideAirbnb data drop off a cliff starting in March 2020, making it one of the clearest signals in our entire dataset. At the same time, the city freezes RSO rent increases through early 2024, which shows up as unusually stable rent burden patterns in our ACS data (S2503). [15]

Crisis & Disruption Mid-2020

Guest Priorities Shift

Research from this period shows guests increasingly choosing entire-unit bookings, self-check-in, and location privacy, all of which support physical distancing. We can actually see this shift in our data through the room_type field by comparing the share of entire-home listings against private rooms and shared rooms before and after COVID. [16]

2021–22
Platform & Market 2021–2022

Uneven Post-Pandemic Recovery

Review activity in our InsideAirbnb data rebounds, but the post-pandemic market looks different. The combined effects of the ordinance, enforcement, and shifting economics mean some neighborhoods recover faster than others. When we overlay review recovery patterns with ACS tract-level demographics (DP05 race/ethnicity, S1901 income), we can observe which communities' housing markets rebounded as tourist commodities and which did not.

Crisis & Disruption Nov 2021–Oct 2022

The Enforcement Gap

City data reveals a stark enforcement gap: more than 2,000 non-compliant short-term rentals were advertised each month, yet only 27 fines were issued across the entire year. The license field's missingness patterns in our data are a proxy for this enforcement failure. [17]

2023
Data & Research 2023

Housing Justice Data Lab Founded

Murray Cox founds the nonprofit Housing Justice Data Lab (HJDL) to keep InsideAirbnb running and ensure the ongoing data collection our project depends on. It's worth noting that our primary dataset comes from an advocacy project, not a neutral archive, and acknowledging that context is part of how we critically engage with the data. [7]

2025
Data & Research 2025

Our Full Dataset Window

Our InsideAirbnb review data runs through December 2025, giving us a 16-year window of observable activity stretching back to May 2009. We pair this with ACS 5-Year Estimates at the census-tract level, including DP05 (demographics), S0101 (age/sex), S1901 (income), S1501 (education), and S2503 (housing cost burden). Together, these let us trace platform growth, regulatory disruption, COVID impacts, and post-pandemic recovery against the long-term demographic and socioeconomic makeup of LA's communities. [4] [18]
