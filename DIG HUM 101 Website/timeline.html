Background

Timeline

This timeline traces key events that shaped the data we analyze in this project. Each entry either describes something directly observable in our InsideAirbnb and American Community Survey datasets, or documents a policy, crisis, or market shift whose effects are visible in the spatial and temporal patterns our data records.

Platform & Market Regulation & Policy Crisis & Disruption Data & Research
1978–79
Regulation & Policy 1978–1979

LA Enacts the Rent Stabilization Ordinance

Los Angeles caps rent increases for units built on or before October 1978 — approximately 624,000 units across the city. The RSO is central to our analysis because the Home-Sharing Ordinance later bans all RSO units from short-term rental use. In our InsideAirbnb listings data, the absence of RSO-protected units from legal Airbnb supply helps explain why listing density concentrates in certain tracts and not others. [1]

1985
Regulation & Policy 1985

California Passes the Ellis Act

Landlords gain the right to evict tenants and withdraw properties from the rental market. Since 2001, over 27,000 rent-controlled units in LA have been removed through Ellis evictions — some converted to Airbnb rentals. This connects the housing loss measured in our ACS data (S2503 housing cost burden, DP05 demographic shifts) to the short-term rental supply visible in InsideAirbnb. [2] [3]

2009
Data & Research May 2009

Earliest LA Review in Our Dataset

The earliest guest review in our InsideAirbnb Los Angeles reviews.csv appears, marking the first observable trace of Airbnb activity in our dataset. At this point, LA's short-term rental market is negligible — but this timestamp anchors the start of our 16-year analysis window. [4]

2012–14
Platform & Market 2012–2014

Airbnb Explodes Across Los Angeles

Review activity in our InsideAirbnb data accelerates sharply as Airbnb expands into Los Angeles. Venice, Santa Monica, Hollywood, and Downtown emerge as early listing hotspots — the same neighborhoods that appear as high-density clusters in our spatial analysis. By 2014, LA is one of Airbnb's largest U.S. markets. [5] [6]

2015
Data & Research 2015

InsideAirbnb Launches

Murray Cox, an Australian-American community artist and data activist, creates InsideAirbnb.com to provide publicly accessible data on Airbnb's impact on residential housing. The project begins scraping and publishing listing-level data for cities worldwide, including Los Angeles — creating the primary dataset our project relies on for listing location, room type, host behavior, pricing, and review activity. [7]

2016–18
Platform & Market 2016–2018

Listings Nearly Double

The number of short-term Airbnb listings in LA nearly doubles. In our data, this period shows the highest concentration of new host registrations (via host_since dates in listings.csv) and accelerating review volume. Scholars document this as the peak of unregulated Airbnb expansion in LA, with housing advocates linking platform growth to rising rents and reduced housing availability — dynamics our ACS income (S1901) and housing cost burden (S2503) data contextualizes at the tract level. [8] [9] [10]

2018
Regulation & Policy December 11, 2018

Home-Sharing Ordinance Passed

The LA City Council unanimously passes the Home-Sharing Ordinance (LAMC § 12.22 A.32). Key provisions that directly shape our dataset: hosts may only rent their primary residence; all hosts must register and pay a fee; short-term rentals are capped at 120 days per year; and RSO units are banned entirely. The license field in our InsideAirbnb listings data — often blank or missing — reflects the ordinance's uneven compliance. [11]

2019
Regulation & Policy July 1, 2019

Ordinance Takes Effect

The Home-Sharing Ordinance takes effect. The registration portal opens and hosts must display their permit number on all listings. In our InsideAirbnb data, the license field begins to populate for some listings during this period, while many remain blank — a pattern we map to examine where compliance is concentrated versus absent.

Regulation & Policy November 1, 2019

Active Enforcement Begins

Non-registered listings face removal from platforms. One analysis estimates a 78% decrease in listings between 2019 and 2022. In our data, this regulatory shock is visible as a drop in active listing counts and a shift in the geographic distribution of remaining listings. [12] [13]

Data & Research July–December 2019

The Pre-Disruption Baseline

InsideAirbnb scrapes LA listing data in mid- and late 2019, capturing approximately 35,000+ active listings. This snapshot serves as the critical pre-disruption baseline in our analysis — the last window before both enforcement and the pandemic reshape the market. Review activity in our dataset peaks during this period. [4] [14]

2020
Crisis & Disruption March 2020

COVID-19 Shuts Down LA Tourism

California's stay-at-home orders shut down LA's tourism economy. In our InsideAirbnb review data, monthly review counts drop sharply beginning in March 2020 — one of the clearest temporal signals in our dataset. Simultaneously, the city freezes RSO rent increases through early 2024, a policy reflected in the stable rent burden patterns in our ACS data (S2503). [15]

Crisis & Disruption Mid-2020

Guest Priorities Shift

Research shows guest priorities shift toward entire-unit bookings, self-check-in, and location privacy — attributes enabling physical distancing. In our data, this is observable through the room_type field: the share of entire-home listings relative to private rooms and shared rooms can be compared across pre- and post-COVID snapshots. [16]

2021–22
Platform & Market 2021–2022

Uneven Post-Pandemic Recovery

Review activity in our InsideAirbnb data rebounds, but the post-pandemic market looks different. The combined effects of the ordinance, enforcement, and shifting economics mean some neighborhoods recover faster than others. When we overlay review recovery patterns with ACS tract-level demographics (DP05 race/ethnicity, S1901 income), we can observe which communities' housing markets rebounded as tourist commodities and which did not.

Crisis & Disruption Nov 2021–Oct 2022

The Enforcement Gap

City data reveals a stark enforcement gap: more than 2,000 non-compliant short-term rentals were advertised each month, yet only 27 fines were issued across the entire year. The license field's missingness patterns in our data are a proxy for this enforcement failure. [17]

2023
Data & Research 2023

Housing Justice Data Lab Founded

Murray Cox establishes the nonprofit Housing Justice Data Lab (HJDL) to continue maintaining InsideAirbnb, ensuring the ongoing data collection our project depends on. Understanding who maintains our primary dataset and why — as an advocacy project, not a neutral archive — is part of our data critique. [7]

2025
Data & Research 2025

Our Full Dataset Window

Our InsideAirbnb review data extends through December 2025, giving the project a 16-year window of observable activity (May 2009–December 2025). Combined with ACS 5-Year Estimates at the census-tract level — DP05 (demographics), S0101 (age/sex), S1901 (income), S1501 (education), S2503 (housing cost burden) — this allows us to trace platform growth, regulatory disruption, COVID impacts, and post-pandemic recovery, all layered against the long-term demographic and socioeconomic structure of LA's communities. [4] [18]
